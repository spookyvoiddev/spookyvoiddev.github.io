Battle Brothers – The Tale of Sun’s End Company

Created on October 28, 2019

2019   ·   Battle brothers   Game Design   Gaming   video game

Battle Brothers is a game that released in 2017 by Overhype Studios. You take the role as the leader of a band of no-so-merry men who set out to become a mercenary company, doing contracts for the various rulers of villages and nobles in whatever randomly generated world you’re put into. An unforgiving game reminiscent of Darkest Dungeon, Battle Brothers will punish you. Hard. Your troops will die, RNG will fail you, and you will be frustrated and angry. You will learn the names of your men who run away at the slightest inclination that you will lose the battle, the names of men who will fight to the last breath, and the names of the men who will become your saviors when the day looks lost. This is the tale of my playthrough, the Sun’s End Company.

When you begin the game, you have a plethora of options to pick from when it comes to choosing a starting point. The default is that you are the second-in-command of a company whose leader has just died, making you the first in command. While this is a good starting point, I decided to choose a more sinister origin story. I chose to be a cult follower of Davkul, an elusive figure within the Battle Brother’s world. This background made it so that my initial company is made up of fellow cultists, and that there are small chances that a cultist will volunteer to join us (very good for a company with little money.) So I chose this origin, chose the bloody sun as our banner, and set off! Quickly realizing the harsh world of Battle Brothers.

My first contract? Escort a caravan a few days North and I’ll get paid five hundred gold. I try to push for more, and he won’t budge. Fine, I won’t lose the contract by trying to haggle even more. We quickly leave with the caravan, and all seems fine. Until we are raided in the night by a group of brigands. This is when I realized that caravan hands do nothing for you, and you must do your best to make sure none of your troops get surrounded. Being the inexperienced player that I am, I quickly sent in my first man in, armed with a two-handed flail and cultist mask and rags, I figured he would quickly bash the skull in of the first enemy that dared run-up to this intimidating figure of death. Unbeknownst to me at the time, this man had a weak resolve. As soon as his turn ended and the enemy moved up onto him, he quickly raised his white flag and tried to make a break for it.

Now, if you’ve played DnD or tabletop games in general, you’ll know what an attack of opportunity is. Generally, they’re restricted to one per character (unless you have feats, but let’s not get into that.) Essentially, if you attempt to move out of an enemy’s zone of control, they get a free attack on you. In Battle Brothers, if that attack hits, you don’t move AND they hit you. If you attempt to move again, they get another attack, rinse, repeat.

When a troop in Battle Brothers attempts to retreat, they will use their entire turn attempting to move out of the zone of control. Now you can see why this is a problem when someone is surrounded by three brigands. He tries to leave, gets hit twice, tries to leave again, and then is finally smashed into the ground by a club, not unlike the shape of a baseball bat.

So one of my company is dead already, and the third round has yet to pass. I am left with three more poorly equipped, scared, starving, and death-seeking cultists. I decide the best course of action is to tactfully run as fast as I can into the other direction, hoping that perhaps the caravan is strong enough to face the brigands on their own. They were not. My troops quickly ran for their lives, abandoning the contract and leaving the caravan to die.

At this point, I was pretty upset. Not only had I lost my strongest man, but I had also failed the contract, dropped my reputation from the village I left it with, and I was now out of money. I decided that the best course of action was, to, in fact, start another glorious mission of attempting to escort a caravan to another town. These are easily the most useful missions early on as food is provided for your company, the caravan automoves, and the chance to be attacked is very low if you do the ones that only require you to go to one or two towns over.

After many caravan contracts, delivery contracts, and exploration contracts, I finally felt strong enough to raid a bandit camp. With my company now seven strong, armed with flails, shields, two giant battleaxes, and a spear, I felt that I could handle a small group of brigands. I had learned from my previous mistakes and I sent in the shieldbearers first, making a line of men holding shieldwalls as my spearman poked at the enemy from behind the line of cover. My men with axes quickly surrounded the enemy and used the aptly named ability “Split Man,” to indeed, split a man.

I had done it, the enemy was breaking, their troops were scattered, and my troops were high in morale. They raised our company banner and rallied to it. I could hear their shouts and battle cries as they charged in. My successor to the two-handed flail bearer from earlier, named Borat “Skull-Drinker” charged in and provided justice for his fallen brother, quickly smashing in the body of a bandit. The enemy soon raised their white flag and ran, leaving their equipment and goods behind. The day was mine, and Sun’s End Company celebrated by going to the nearest village, tending to their wounds at the temple, and getting absurdly drunk at the local tavern.

Sun’s End Company continues to live to this day, accepting cultists into its ranks, converting those who choose to accept the gift of Davkul, and beating up monks and all those who refuse him. They raise their banner high with the bleeding sun, and their men are tough, proved in combat, and bonded together with the pain of their fallen brothers.
